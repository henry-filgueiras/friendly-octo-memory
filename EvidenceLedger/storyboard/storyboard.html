Annotated storyboard

Evidence Ledger turns competing narratives into an inspectable evidence map.

Instead of making certainty look effortless, it keeps support, contradiction, source quality, and missing evidence visible in one local-only reasoning surface.

Claims and sources Support vs contradiction Source reliability Gap detection Plain-English explanations

How to read it

1. Write the claims down Don’t start from consensus. Start from the actual hypotheses in play.
2. Wire in evidence explicitly Every source either supports, contradicts, or merely mentions a claim.
3. Let the pressure surface The app pulls contested claims and thin gaps forward automatically.

Step 1

The main claims view shows where the narrative is carrying real weight.

Claims are ranked by importance and evidence pressure, so the discussion starts with the issues that matter and already have signal attached.

Verdicts stay challengeable “Supported” is derived from visible inputs, not hidden judgment.
Score rails show the tension Support, contradiction, and mentions stay visible instead of collapsing into one number.

Step 2

Contested claims show where the story is still fighting itself.

This keeps disagreement explicit. A tidy retrospective often hides the most important unresolved claim.

Both sides have weight The deploy hypothesis looks plausible until you place it next to the counter-evidence.

Step 3

Gap analysis turns “we should look into that” into a concrete next move.

Important claims with thin coverage bubble up fast, so the model can tell you what evidence is missing next.

Thin claims are visible risk People still act on them even when the ledger says the evidence is weak.

Step 4

The matrix shows which sources are actually carrying each claim.

This is the quick read for single-source fragility, contradiction clusters, and source overreach.

Structure beats intuition One glance shows whether the story is broad-based or leaning too hard on one artifact.
